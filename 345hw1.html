The design of was an attempt at randomized complete block design. The experimental units were male X.maculatus, of which there were twelve total. There was one treatment factor, namely was the type of sword that was surgically applied to the fish, and there were two levels: transparent sword and yellow sword. Each block was a pair of male fish, chosen for similar size and coloration As per randomized complete block design, within each pair of male fish, one male fish was randomly assigned a transparent sword and the other male fish was assigned a yellow sword. The observational unit was not the male pair of fish, but the swimming pattern of a female fish, or, more broadly, the pair of male fish with a female fish in a specialized holding tank. The observation recorded was the time (in seconds) the female spent on the side of her compartment nearest the transparent finned-male, as well as the time spent in the area nearest the yellow finned-male. The experiment was not exactly a randomized complete block design because, for each pair of male fish, there were several (between 9 and 16) observations taken from different female fish. These observations are not independent, and thus pseudo-replicates, because they use different female fish, but the same 2-male-fish block. The author inappropriately treats each of these observations as independent replicates, while it could be argued that there is only one independent observation per pair of male fish.

To produce a 95% confidence interval, we need both an estimated mean difference, and an estimated variance of our mean difference. I see two key issues. This results in:

mean(ddply(grav, .(trial), summarize, m = mean(est))$m)

The variance of this estimate, assuming that errors within different series are independant, would be: \[ var(\bar{X}) = var(\bar{X_1})+ var(\bar{X_2}) + var(\bar{X_3})+ \cdots + var(bar{X_8}) \] \[ = \frac{var(\sum{x_{1,i}})}{n_{1}^2}+\frac{var(\sum{x_{2,i}})}{n_{2}^2}+\frac{var(\sum{x_{3,i}})}{n_{3}^2} + \cdots + \frac{var(\sum{x_{3,i}})}{n_{8}^2} \]

This requires estimating the variance of the sum of observations within series, which (by the plot), clearly have a dependance structure.
